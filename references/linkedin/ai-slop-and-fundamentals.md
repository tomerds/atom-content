# Writing LinkedIn Posts With AI Without the AI Slop

General craft for AI-assisted LinkedIn writing, gathered from creators and writing
experts outside Atom. Backs up and deepens the Stanley playbook
(`playbooks/linkedin/playbook.md`). On any conflict, the playbook wins.

By 2026 the LinkedIn audience has learned to spot AI-written posts and tune them
out. The goal here is not to "beat detectors." It is to write posts that sound like
a specific human had a specific thought, because that is what earns attention now.

---

## 1. The AI tells (what flags a post as AI-written)

The 2026 audience actively calls these out. Avoid them.

**Vocabulary that spikes in AI text:** delve, landscape, tapestry, transformative,
unleash, unlock, elevate, leverage, empower, navigate, synergy, pivotal, testament,
realm, game-changer, crucial, vital, "moreover," "furthermore," "additionally,"
"it's important to note," "key takeaway." Most of these already appear on the
playbook's avoid-list (Part 7.3); this widens it.

**Cliché openers:** "In today's fast-paced / ever-evolving world," "As a
professional in the space," "In an era where…" These signal a template before the
reader hits the second line.

**Punctuation and formatting tells:**

- Em dashes used for drama (already an absolute rule in the playbook: never).
- An emoji on every line, or emoji used as bullets (🚀 💡 🔥). Decorative emoji
  blocks read as generated.
- Perfectly uniform formatting where every paragraph is the same shape.

**Structural tells:**

- Rule-of-three everywhere ("faster, smarter, better"). One is fine; a post built
  on stacked triplets feels manufactured.
- The "It's not just X. It's Y." construction (and its cousin "That's not a
  strategy. That's survival mode."). The playbook flags this in Part 5.3; the wider
  world reads it as a top AI tell.
- Sentences that announce themselves: "Here's the thing," "Here's why," "The truth
  is," or that end on "That's the real [X]."
- The achievement-post formula: announce the milestone, express gratitude, close on
  a motivational line. Grammatically flawless, personality-free.

Sources: [Hunting the Muse](https://huntingthemuse.net/library/how-to-tell-if-writing-is-ai),
[Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-30/chatgpt-written-linkedin-posts-have-users-analyzing-emojis-other-ai-signs),
[TeamLewis](https://www.teamlewis.com/magazine/5-common-mistakes-to-avoid-in-ai-generated-linkedin-posts/).

## 2. The deeper why: statistical fingerprints

Two measurable patterns explain most of the tells above. Knowing them is more
useful than memorizing a word list, because they tell you what to fix.

**Perplexity (word choice):** language models pick the statistically likely next
word, so AI text reads as "smooth" and predictable. Humans reach for the less
expected, more specific word. Concrete nouns and real detail raise perplexity in
the way that reads as human.

**Burstiness (sentence rhythm):** AI tends to cluster sentences in the 15-25 word
band, so everything is mid-length. Human writing swings, a three-word sentence next
to a long winding one. Vary sentence length on purpose.

**The fix is structural, not cosmetic.** Swapping "utilize" for "use" or "however"
for "but" changes neither perplexity nor burstiness. You have to restructure
sentences, break the uniform rhythm, and add detail only you would know.

Sources: [Phrasly](https://phrasly.ai/blog/humanize-ai-text/).

## 3. Humanization and editing technique

How to actually use AI so the output doesn't read as AI.

- **Bring the insight yourself.** Know the specific, contrarian, or useful point
  before you open the tool. With only a topic, AI defaults to generic advice.
- **Feed it raw, messy input, not a topic.** A voice-note transcript, three bullet
  fragments about something you noticed, a rambling paragraph. Then have it shape
  that into a post. Grounding it in real material is what keeps it specific.
- **Style-prime it.** Paste three to five real past posts so it matches your
  sentence length, vocabulary, and cadence, rather than the LinkedIn-average voice.
- **Treat AI as a first-draft accelerator, not a ghostwriter.** The "80% isn't good
  enough" rule: never publish raw output. Read it once, then close it and edit as
  the editor, not the author.
- **Rewrite the opener by hand,** in words you would actually say out loud.
- **Cut or replace formal connectors.** "Furthermore / Moreover / Additionally"
  become "Plus / On top of that," or just get deleted.
- **Inject specifics only you have:** real numbers, names, the dollar amount, the
  thing that went wrong. Specificity is the hardest thing for a generator to fake.

Sources: [MagicPost](https://magicpost.in/blog/ai-linkedin-posts),
[TeamLewis](https://www.teamlewis.com/magazine/5-common-mistakes-to-avoid-in-ai-generated-linkedin-posts/).

## 4. Hook fundamentals

The opening line does most of the work. LinkedIn shows only the first one to two
lines before "see more," and that is where the reader decides whether to expand.

- **Write the hook first,** then the post. Strong creators draft three to seven
  variations and pick one. Spend real time here.
- **Keep it short.** Aim for one strong sentence or two tight lines, roughly under
  200 characters; shorter hooks tend to win on a mobile feed.
- **Lead with impact, not context.** "I've been thinking a lot about leadership
  lately…" buries the point. Open on the surprising thing.
- **Deliver on the promise.** If the hook sets up a specific insight, story, or
  number, the body must pay it off. Clickbait that disappoints is worse than a flat
  hook.

**Hook frameworks (creator-tested):**

1. Contrarian statement: challenge accepted wisdom.
2. Unexpected number: a specific, slightly surprising figure.
3. Dramatic moment: drop the reader mid-action, no setup.
4. Uncomfortable truth: say the thing everyone thinks but rarely posts.
5. Time-bound transformation: a real change across a stated timeframe.
6. The question everyone's asking: a question the audience already wonders about.
7. Mini case-study preview: tease the concrete result before the method.
8. Unpopular opinion: signal a take most will resist, then argue it.

These map cleanly onto the playbook's four house hooks (stat, situation drop,
unexpected question, contrarian statement). Use the playbook's framing first; reach
here for more variety when a post needs it.

Sources: [FinalLayer](https://finallayer.com/blog/linkedin-hook-frameworks),
[ConnectSafely](https://connectsafely.ai/articles/linkedin-hooks-engagement-guide-2026).

## 5. How this defers to the playbook

This file is the general craft. `playbooks/linkedin/playbook.md` is Atom's specific
voice, audience (research development professionals), product references, and
real-world examples. When they overlap, follow the playbook's wording. When the
playbook is silent (the statistical "why," the wider banned-word list, the extra
hook frameworks), use this. Never let a general rule here flatten Atom's house
voice.
