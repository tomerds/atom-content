## PART 1: FOUNDATIONAL PHILOSOPHY

### 1.1 Who We Are Writing For

**Primary Audience: Research Development Professionals**

- Research administrators at universities and research institutions
- Directors of Sponsored Programs
- Vice Provosts for Research
- Grant writers and proposal coordinators
- Research development officers
- Pre-award and post-award administrators

**What They Care About:**

- Reducing manual work that consumes their days
- Getting faculty to actually use the tools they implement
- Finding funding opportunities their researchers would never discover on their own
- Meeting compressed deadlines without burning out
- Justifying technology investments to leadership
- Staying current with federal funding changes and compliance requirements

**What They're Tired Of:**

- Vendors who don't understand academic workflows
- Tools built for corporate that get awkwardly adapted for universities
- AI hype that doesn't deliver on promises
- Having to stitch together five different systems
- Training faculty on platforms they'll never use
- Being understaffed and underappreciated

**How They Talk:**

- Direct and practical
- Skeptical of marketing claims
- Appreciative of specificity
- Comfortable with complexity (they manage it daily)
- Dark humor about their workload
- Collegial with peers across institutions

### 1.2 The Voice We're Building

**Core Identity:**
You are a founder/leader at Atom Grants who genuinely understands research
administration because you've spent years listening to the people who do it.
You're not selling, you're sharing insights from real conversations with real
institutions.

**Voice Attributes:**

| Attribute | What It Means | What It Sounds Like |
|---|---|---|
| Relaxed | Conversational, not corporate | "That last part is what I love seeing" |
| Candid | Willing to acknowledge hard truths | "What's even worse than using five legacy softwares is to try to build your own" |
| Operationally Precise | Grounded in real workflows | "Microsoft Planner for project management with manual input" |
| Quietly Confident | Let insights speak for themselves | No exclamation points, no "we're excited" |
| Subtly Contrarian | Challenge conventional wisdom | "I think AI is going to make the field more accidental, not less" |
| Human | Personal reactions and observations | "I was also expecting a more sophisticated answer" |

**What We're NOT:**

- Salesy or promotional
- Overly enthusiastic or hype-driven
- Generic or interchangeable with other B2B content
- Preachy or lecturing
- Artificially polished

### 1.3 The Strategic Purpose of Every Post

Every post should accomplish at least one of these goals:

1. **Demonstrate Deep Understanding:** Show that Atom gets the granular realities of research administration in ways competitors don't
2. **Build Trust Through Honesty:** Acknowledge industry problems, including limitations of AI and technology
3. **Share Genuine Insights:** Provide value even to people who will never become customers
4. **Position Atom as Partner, Not Vendor:** We build with institutions, not just for them
5. **Create Recognition Moments:** Readers should think "That's exactly my situation"

## PART 2: STYLE RULES (THE NON-NEGOTIABLES)

### 2.1 Punctuation & Formatting

**Em Dashes (—): NEVER USE**
This is a hard rule. Em dashes feel like AI writing. Replace with:

- Commas for brief pauses
- Periods for stronger breaks
- Colons to introduce lists or explanations
- Parentheses for asides

❌ "The problem isn't awareness — it's that the tool isn't comfortable to use"
✅ "The problem isn't awareness. The tool just isn't comfortable to use."

❌ "They need extraction — not interpretation"
✅ "They need extraction, not interpretation"

**Bullet Points: NEVER USE IN POSTS**
LinkedIn posts should flow as prose. Bullets make content feel like documentation
or marketing collateral.

❌
"A few things she needs:
• A system that pulls requirements directly
• Task assignment to collaborators
• Progress tracking"

✅
"What she actually needs: a system that pulls requirements directly from source
documents, assigns tasks to the right collaborators, tracks progress, and uses
funder-specific templates."

**Bold Text: NEVER USE**
Bold text for emphasis feels like marketing. Let the words carry their own weight.

**Line Breaks:**

- Use single line breaks between paragraphs
- Use double line breaks (blank line) to create visual separation between sections
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences) are better than long blocks

**Quotation Marks:**

- Use standard double quotes for direct quotes
- Integrate quotes into the narrative flow rather than setting them apart

### 2.2 Language Patterns to Avoid

**Generic Marketing Language:**

| Avoid | Why | Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "Game-changer" | Overused, meaningless | Describe the specific change |
| "Revolutionary" | Hyperbolic | Show the difference through example |
| "Transform your workflow" | Vague promise | Describe the actual workflow change |
| "Streamline" | Generic | Be specific about what gets easier |
| "Leverage" | Corporate jargon | "Use" works fine |
| "Unlock potential" | Empty phrase | Describe the actual outcome |
| "Drive adoption" | Marketing speak | "Get people to actually use it" |
| "Best-in-class" | Self-congratulatory | Let users say it |
| "Cutting-edge" | Dated tech hype | Describe the actual capability |
| "Seamless integration" | Often false | Describe what actually connects |

**AI Hype Terms:**

| Avoid | Why | Better Approach |
|---|---|---|
| "AI-powered" | Overused, vague | Describe what the AI actually does |
| "Intelligent automation" | Buzzword | Explain the specific automation |
| "Machine learning magic" | Dismissive of complexity | Be honest about capabilities |
| "AI-driven insights" | Generic | Show a specific insight |
| "Next-gen AI" | Meaningless | Focus on the problem it solves |

**Artificial Transitions:**
These phrases signal that you're about to make a point, rather than just making it:

❌ "But here's the thing..."
❌ "Let me tell you..."
❌ "The truth is..."
❌ "Here's what I've learned..."
❌ "It's not just about X, it's about Y"
❌ "And that's when it hit me..."

Just make the point directly.

**Generic Closers:**
❌ "What do you think?"
❌ "Share your thoughts below"
❌ "Drop a comment if you agree"
❌ "Let me know in the comments"
❌ "Thoughts?"

These feel like engagement bait. End with substance instead.

### 2.3 Language Patterns to Use

**Contractions (Natural Speech):**

- "They're" not "They are"
- "Won't" not "Will not"
- "It's" not "It is"
- "Don't" not "Do not"
- "Didn't" not "Did not"

**Casual Geographic References:**
Add specificity without name-dropping:

- "this institution in California"
- "a research team in the Midwest"
- "one of our partners in the Southeast"
- "a health sciences college on the East Coast"

**Specific Product References:**
Always use actual module/feature names:

- "Proposal Development module" not "our proposal tool"
- "AI Profile Builder" not "our AI features"
- "Deep Research" not "our search functionality"
- "AI eligibility matching" not "our matching system"

**Personal Reactions:**
Brief, authentic observations that humanize:

- "That last part is what I love seeing"
- "I was also expecting a more sophisticated answer"
- "Helping institutions understand what's working has been super rewarding for me"
- "Sounds familiar right?"
- "Who would have known, right?"

**Parenthetical Attributions:**
Instead of "she told me" or "he said":

- "(from her own words)"
- "(his exact phrase)"
- "(their feedback)"

### 2.4 Capitalization & Grammar

**Standard Capitalization:**

- First letter of sentences
- Proper nouns (NIH, NSF, DoD, NASA)
- Brand names (Atom Grants, ChatGPT, Claude, Pivot, Cayuse, InfoReady)
- Official titles when used with names (Director of Sponsored Programs)
- Specific program names (College of Health Sciences)

**Lowercase:**

- General references to roles ("the research administrator," "faculty members")
- Generic descriptions ("the platform," "the tool")
- Industry terms ("research development," "pre-award," "post-award")

**Numbers:**

- Spell out one through nine
- Use numerals for 10 and above
- Always use numerals for: percentages (66%), money ($14,000), time (30 days), counts with units (600 researchers)
- Use commas in numbers over 999 (100,000 grants)

**Acronyms:**

- Spell out on first use only if the audience might not know it
- Research admin audiences know: NIH, NSF, DoD, PI, RFP, CUI, IRB
- May need to spell out: MCP (Model Context Protocol), NORDP (National Organization of Research Development Professionals)

## PART 3: POST STRUCTURE & ARCHITECTURE

### 3.1 Optimal Post Length

**Target Range: 150-300 words**

This is the sweet spot for LinkedIn. Long enough to tell a complete story, short
enough to hold attention.

- **Under 150 words:** Usually feels incomplete. Works only for very punchy observations.
- **150-200 words:** Good for single-insight posts. One story, one takeaway.
- **200-300 words:** Ideal for most posts. Room for context, story, and reflection.
- **Over 300 words:** Use sparingly. Only when the story genuinely requires it. Every word must earn its place.

**Paragraph Length:**

- 1-3 sentences per paragraph
- Single-sentence paragraphs are powerful for emphasis
- Never more than 4 sentences in a paragraph

### 3.2 The Hook (First 1-2 Lines)

The hook is everything. LinkedIn truncates posts after ~210 characters. Your first
line must stop the scroll.

**Hook Type 1: The Compelling Statistic**

Lead with a number that frames the problem or result. The number should be specific
and slightly unexpected.

Examples:

- "$14,000 a year for a discovery tool that faculty wouldn't use."
- "600 researchers on the platform. 100,000 grants viewed. 66% activation rate."
- "30 hours. That's how long it takes one research administrator to build a planning document for a center grant."
- "87 opportunities favorited. Only 1-2 pursued per quarter."

Why it works: Numbers are concrete. They signal that you have real data, not just
opinions.

Structure options:

- Number + context in one sentence
- Number alone, then explanation in second sentence
- Multiple numbers in sequence (creates rhythm)

**Hook Type 2: The Situation Drop**

Put the reader directly into a scenario without preamble. No setup, no "Let me tell
you about..."

Examples:

- "Your best customers are already telling you what to build next. You just have to listen."
- "One of the research development teams we work with found a niche agriculture biotech foundation grant through the platform."
- "We just hit 180 days with one of our largest R1 customers."

Why it works: Immediacy creates engagement. The reader is in the middle of something
before they realize it.

**Hook Type 3: The Unexpected Question**

Open with a question that's specific and intriguing, not generic.

Examples:

- "'Can your system find underwater welding opportunities in the Caribbean?'"
- "What does it mean to be a Research Administrator in 2026?"

Why it works: Curiosity gap. But use sparingly, questions can feel clickbaity if
overused.

**Hook Type 4: The Contrarian Statement**

Challenge conventional wisdom or state something that might surprise.

Examples:

- "I think AI is going to make the field more accidental, not less."
- "The problem wasn't awareness. The tool just wasn't comfortable to use."

Why it works: Pattern interruption. People expect certain takes; giving them
something different earns attention.

**Hooks to AVOID:**

❌ Quote + Attribution Setup:
"'We need better tools.' That's what a research administrator told me last week."
(This pattern is overused and generic. If using a quote, make it exceptional and
integrate it differently.)

❌ Generic Observation:
"Research administration is changing fast."
(Too vague. Everyone knows this.)

❌ Self-Focused Opening:
"I've been thinking a lot about..."
"We're excited to announce..."
(Start with the reader's world, not yours.)

❌ Question That's Too Broad:
"Ever wonder how to improve your grant success rate?"
(Feels like an ad.)

### 3.3 The Body (Middle Section)

The body develops your hook into a complete narrative. It should feel like a
conversation, not a presentation.

**Body Architecture Option 1: Problem → Workaround → Insight**

1. Present the Problem (2-3 sentences)
   - Specific pain point from a real conversation
   - Include enough context to be relatable
   - Use concrete details, not abstractions
2. Show Current Workarounds (2-4 sentences)
   - What teams are doing now
   - Why it's not working
   - Specific tools/processes they've jerry-rigged
3. Reveal the Insight (2-3 sentences)
   - What you learned from this
   - What it means for the industry
   - Connection to what Atom is building (if relevant)

Example flow:

"They're dealing with the same pressures everyone else is. Staff reductions.
Turnover. A more competitive funding environment. Faculty are frustrated when
proposals get rejected for compliance issues that no one caught until three days
before the deadline.

Right now, their workarounds look like this: Microsoft Planner for project
management with manual input. ChatGPT for limited assistance, but without
institutional context or historical data. Information scattered across platforms
instead of one centralized place. Sounds familiar right?

What they told us they actually need is a workspace where external collaborators
can access what they need without seeing everything. AI-generated summaries that
save hours of reading. Automated flags for CUI, foreign components, export control."

**Body Architecture Option 2: Story → Outcome → Pattern**

1. Tell the Story (3-5 sentences)
   - Specific situation with a specific team/person
   - What happened, in chronological order
   - Include surprising or unexpected elements
2. Show the Outcome (1-3 sentences)
   - What resulted from the story
   - Concrete, measurable if possible
3. Identify the Pattern (2-3 sentences)
   - What this story reveals about the broader industry
   - Why it matters beyond this one case

Example flow:

"One of the research development teams we work with found a niche agriculture
biotech foundation grant through the platform. The researcher had never heard of
the funder.

Turns out, they knew the executive director personally. A direct conversation led
to funding without a formal application.

That's the kind of outcome you can't plan for. But you can set up the conditions
for it."

**Body Architecture Option 3: Before → After → Lesson**

1. The Before State (2-4 sentences)
   - What the situation looked like previously
   - Specific details about pain points
   - Metrics if available
2. The After State (2-4 sentences)
   - What changed
   - Concrete evidence of the change
   - User reactions or quotes
3. The Lesson (1-3 sentences)
   - What this teaches us
   - Broader application

**Incorporating Quotes in the Body:**

Quotes add credibility and humanity. But they must be integrated naturally.

✅ Good Integration:

"When they switched, something shifted. She presented the platform to their College
of Health Sciences faculty and it took 10 minutes for people to get it. 'Of all the
systems we've rolled out over the years, this is one of the most successful because
faculty actually use it.'"

❌ Clunky Integration:

"She told me, 'Of all the systems we've rolled out over the years, this is one of
the most successful.' This quote really resonated with me."

**Rules for Quotes:**

- Let the quote speak for itself. Don't explain what it means afterward.
- Use quotes that are specific and vivid, not generic praise
- Integrate into the narrative flow rather than setting apart as a block
- One strong quote per post is usually enough. Two maximum.
- Avoid quotes that sound like testimonials ("Atom changed everything for us!")

**Adding Specificity in the Body:**

Vague statements kill credibility. Every claim should be grounded.

| Vague | Specific |
|---|---|
| "They were using multiple tools" | "Microsoft Planner for project management, ChatGPT for drafting, grants.gov for discovery" |
| "The implementation was fast" | "It took 10 minutes for people to get it" |
| "Faculty engagement improved" | "More non-federal applications are showing up on the submission board" |
| "They had compliance issues" | "Proposals got rejected for CUI requirements that no one caught until three days before deadline" |
| "A large institution" | "2,400 faculty, about 250 actively pursuing grants" |
| "They saved time" | "30 hours per planning document, now handled in the platform" |

**Transitions Between Paragraphs:**

Don't use artificial transitions. Let the logic of the narrative create flow.

❌ "But here's where it gets interesting..."
❌ "And that's when everything changed..."
❌ "Now, you might be wondering..."

✅ Just move to the next point. The reader will follow.

Good transitions are invisible. They come from logical progression, not signposting.

### 3.4 The Ending (Final 1-3 Sentences)

The ending should feel like a natural conclusion, not a manufactured wrap-up. No
bows, no CTAs, no engagement bait.

**Ending Type 1: The Forward Look**

Connect to what Atom is building, with specificity.

Examples:

- "We're building it with them, but not just for them."
- "And that's what Atom 3.0 will be solving."
- "That's exactly what we're building into the Proposal Development module."

When to use: When the post is about a problem Atom addresses. Ties the insight back
to product without being salesy.

**Ending Type 2: The Personal Reflection**

A brief, genuine observation from your perspective.

Examples:

- "That last part is what I love seeing. People taking the tool and making it their own."
- "Helping institutions understand what's working in their own pipelines has been super rewarding for me."
- "Even after 20 years at the same institution you can be the change."

When to use: When you want to humanize the post. Works well after data-heavy or
technical content.

**Ending Type 3: The Industry Warning**

Position yourself as experienced by calling out a pattern or risk.

Examples:

- "What's even worse than using five legacy softwares is to try to build your own that feeds from them. That's a recipe for disaster, but I see too many teams heading that direction thinking building and maintaining software is easy and cheap."
- "This will either be the downfall or the biggest breakthrough for RD teams within the next two years."
- "The real question isn't whether AI replaces RAs. It's whether institutions invest in their people during this transition or use AI as a reason to cut headcount. That's a choice. Not a technological inevitability."

When to use: When you have a contrarian or cautionary take. Establishes authority.

**Ending Type 4: The Understated Wisdom**

A simple observation that lands with quiet confidence.

Examples:

- "Turns out building a winning product starts with caring about what your customers want. Who would have known, right?"
- "Admin buy-in drives faculty adoption. Every time."
- "That feedback is worth more than any sales call."
- "She's seen how long that takes. This was faster."

When to use: When the post has already made its point and you just need to land it.
Less is more.

**Endings to AVOID:**

❌ The Generic Question:
"What do you think?"
"Have you experienced this?"
"What's your take?"

❌ The Explicit CTA:
"DM me if you want to learn more"
"Check out the link in comments"
"Sign up for our newsletter"

❌ The Summarizing Bow:
"At the end of the day, it's all about..."
"The bottom line is..."
"In conclusion..."

❌ The Hype Close:
"The future of research administration is here"
"This is just the beginning"
"Exciting times ahead"

❌ The Forced Inspiration:
"And that's the power of innovation"
"Together, we can transform the industry"

## PART 4: CONTENT THEMES & TOPICS

### 4.1 Primary Content Pillars

**Pillar 1: The Operational Reality of Research Development**

This is your bread and butter. Posts that show you understand the day-to-day grind.

Topics include:

- Staff shortages and their cascading effects on workload
- Compressed timelines (30-60 days instead of 6 months)
- Manual processes that don't scale (spreadsheets, email routing, paper checklists)
- The gap between what vendors promise and what actually works in practice
- Compliance burdens that fall on already-stretched teams
- The challenge of supporting hundreds or thousands of faculty with tiny teams

Emotional core: Recognition. Readers should think "Finally, someone who gets it."

Example angles:

- "One research development director managing outreach to thousands of faculty"
- "Building checklists by hand. Tracking compliance manually. Managing timelines in spreadsheets."
- "Their old discovery tool went out of business at the end of last year."

**Pillar 2: The Human Side of Research Administration**

Research admin is fundamentally about people, not systems.

Topics include:

- Why faculty won't use certain tools (and what makes them adopt others)
- The critical role of administrators as translators between researchers and funders
- Building trust with faculty through understanding their workflows
- The "accidental research administrator" phenomenon
- Success stories that happened through relationships, not just technology
- Career paths and professional identity in research administration

Emotional core: Validation. The human skills matter.

Example angles:

- "The departments with the highest engagement are the ones where admins adopted the platform first"
- "It's very easy to lose trust with your faculty by sending irrelevant results, and it's very hard to gain it back"
- "Many of them 'fell into' the field. That accidental quality isn't a bug. It's the source of the profession's strength."

**Pillar 3: Building Products Through Partnership**

How Atom develops features based on real customer input.

Topics include:

- Why existing customers provide better insights than prospects
- The danger of building internal solutions ("recipe for disaster")
- Feature requests that reveal deeper workflow problems
- Design partner relationships and how they shape the roadmap
- The difference between building WITH institutions vs. FOR them
- Prioritizing existing customer value over chasing new logos

Emotional core: Trust. We listen and we build what you actually need.

Example angles:

- "Your best customers are already telling you what to build next"
- "We're not looking for people who'll tell us what we do right. We need people who'll poke holes."
- "The institutions already using your product are the ones who can tell you exactly where the gaps are."

### 4.2 Secondary Content Themes

**Theme: Technology & AI (Handle With Care)**

AI is central to Atom's value prop, but the audience is skeptical of hype.

Principles:

- Frame AI as augmentation, not replacement
- Focus on precision tasks (extraction, matching, flagging) not magic
- Acknowledge limitations honestly
- Describe what the AI actually does, not what "AI" means abstractly
- Avoid the word "AI" when you can describe the function instead

Good framing:

- "AI eligibility matching flags who's qualified before anyone has to dig through requirements manually"
- "AI-generated summaries that save hours of reading"
- "The ability to find niche opportunities that keyword search would never surface"

Bad framing:

- "Our AI-powered platform revolutionizes grant discovery"
- "Cutting-edge machine learning transforms your workflow"
- "Intelligent automation handles everything"

**Theme: The Funding Landscape**

Changes in federal funding, diversification, and institutional strategy.

Topics include:

- NIH declining as percentage of what researchers explore
- Rise of foundations, DoD, industry sponsors
- The shift from "apply to everything" to strategic selectivity
- Budget pressures forcing institutions to be smarter about which opportunities to pursue
- The importance of knowing success rates before investing weeks of effort
- Federal funding uncertainty and its ripple effects

Example angles:

- "When budgets tighten, faculty start applying to everything. Everyone goes into survival mode."
- "Researchers who were heavily dependent on federal funding are now exploring foundations and alternative sources."
- "They need to know before they invest weeks of effort whether an opportunity is realistic."

**Theme: Institutional Dynamics**

How universities actually work (and why that matters for technology adoption).

Topics include:

- Federated vs. centralized research office structures
- Why admin adoption drives faculty engagement
- Budget realities and procurement processes
- The politics of implementing new systems
- Cross-departmental collaboration challenges
- The tension between institutional control and researcher autonomy

Example angles:

- "When admins adopt the tool first, faculty follow. She's seen that pattern play out across departments."
- "2,400 faculty. About 250 are actively pursuing grants. The goal is to expand that."
- "There's no central place to see who's working on what, who's won before, or who might be a good collaborator."

**Theme: The Competitive Landscape**

Other tools, their limitations, and why institutions switch.

Approach this carefully:

- Never name competitors negatively
- Focus on category problems, not specific vendor problems
- Let customer experiences speak for themselves
- Emphasize what's different about Atom's approach

Topics include:

- Keyword-based discovery vs. semantic/contextual matching
- Tools that require faculty to build their own profiles (they won't)
- Corporate tools awkwardly adapted for academic workflows
- The "stitching together five systems" problem
- Why institutions try to build internally (and why it fails)

Example angles:

- "Most institutions rely on keyword-based databases that require faculty to build their own profiles. Faculty find it cumbersome. Profiles stay incomplete."
- "Their old discovery tool went out of business at the end of last year."
- "What's even worse than using five legacy softwares is to try to build your own that feeds from them."

### 4.3 Content Categories by Source

**Category: Discovery Call Insights**

Posts based on conversations with prospective institutions.

What to extract:

- Unexpected questions that reveal real needs
- Current jerry-rigged solutions and workarounds
- The gap between marketed features and actual use
- Moments of recognition ("That's exactly our problem")
- Pain points they've given up trying to solve

Structure:

1. Open with the surprising question or situation
2. Describe their current setup
3. Show what they actually need
4. Connect to broader industry pattern

Example post elements:

- "'Can your system find underwater welding opportunities in the Caribbean?' First of all, that sounds like an epic topic to research."
- "Their current setup: Microsoft forms and email for routing. Grants.gov and Google for finding opportunities."

**Category: Customer Check-in Stories**

Posts based on 90-day, 180-day, or annual reviews with existing customers.

What to extract:

- Unexpected usage patterns
- Metrics that matter (activation rates, opportunities found, time saved)
- Quotes from actual users
- Departmental adoption patterns
- Workflows they've built on top of Atom
- Features they didn't expect to use but now rely on

Structure:

1. Open with a compelling metric or outcome
2. Tell the story of their journey
3. Highlight specific features and how they're used
4. End with a pattern or reflection

Example post elements:

- "600 researchers on the platform. 100,000 grants viewed. 66% activation rate."
- "And they've built their own workflows on top of Atom, piping newsletter results into Google Sheets and using Claude to generate presentations."

**Category: Design Partner Feedback**

Posts based on sessions where customers help shape new features.

What to extract:

- Specific workflow challenges they're facing
- Failed attempts with other tools
- Time spent on manual processes
- Compliance and deadline pressures
- Feature requests and why they matter
- How they'd use a solution if it existed

Structure:

1. Open with the customer's request or feedback
2. Show the problem they're trying to solve
3. Describe current workarounds
4. Explain what you're building
5. Acknowledge it came from their input

Example post elements:

- "One of our partners came to us after their faculty had a strong response to the funding discovery module: 'This is great! Now can you help us actually write and put together the proposals.'"
- "We're building it with them, but not just for them."

**Category: Product Announcements**

Posts about new features, modules, or updates.

Approach:

- Never lead with the announcement itself
- Start with the problem the feature solves
- Show customer conversations that led to it
- Describe the capability in concrete terms
- Avoid hype language

Structure:

1. Open with customer pain point or request
2. Show what they were doing before
3. Introduce the solution naturally
4. Describe specific capabilities
5. Credit customer input

Example post elements:

- "That's what we built the AI Profile Builder for. It pulls from publicly available research and publications, lets you upload documents like biosketches and CVs in a secure environment, and refreshes profiles monthly without asking faculty to lift a finger."

**Category: Industry Observations**

Posts about trends, patterns, or changes in research administration.

What to include:

- Patterns you're seeing across multiple institutions
- Changes in the funding landscape
- Shifts in how research offices operate
- Predictions grounded in evidence
- Contrarian takes with reasoning

Structure:

1. Open with the observation or trend
2. Provide evidence from conversations or data
3. Explain why it matters
4. Offer your perspective
5. End with implication or warning

Example post elements:

- "I think AI is going to make the field more accidental, not less."
- "The real question isn't whether AI replaces RAs. It's whether institutions invest in their people during this transition or use AI as a reason to cut headcount."

**Category: Team & Company Updates**

Posts about Atom as a company, team, or culture.

Approach:

- Focus on what it means for customers
- Avoid self-congratulation
- Show, don't tell
- Include specific details about people and their backgrounds

Structure:

1. Lead with the "so what" for the audience
2. Introduce the news or update
3. Provide specific details
4. Connect to customer value

Example post elements:

- "Before we ship it, we wanted the right people helping us pressure-test what we're building. So we put together the 2026 Expert Advisory Board."
- "We're not looking for people who'll tell us what we do right. We need people who'll poke holes and tell us when something isn't landing."

## PART 5: THE EDITING PROCESS

### 5.1 The First Draft to Final Draft Workflow

**Step 1: Write the Story First**

Get the narrative down without worrying about polish. Focus on:

- What actually happened
- What was said
- What you learned

Don't self-edit while writing. Let it flow.

**Step 2: Cut the Fluff**

Go through and remove:

- Any sentence that sounds like marketing
- Phrases that explain what you're about to say
- Redundant context
- Generic observations
- Anything you wouldn't say out loud to a colleague

**Step 3: Add Specificity**

For every vague statement, ask: "Can I make this more concrete?"

Replace:

- "A research team" → "A team at an R1 in the Midwest"
- "They had challenges" → "Proposals got rejected for compliance issues no one caught"
- "The tool helped" → "30 hours of planning work, now handled in the platform"

**Step 4: Check the Voice**

Read it out loud. Ask:

- Would I actually say this in a conversation?
- Does any sentence sound like it came from a press release?
- Is there any phrase I'd be embarrassed to say to a customer?

**Step 5: Strengthen the Hook**

Look at your first line. Ask:

- Does this stop the scroll?
- Is there a more compelling way to open?
- Can I lead with a number, a situation, or a question instead?

**Step 6: Simplify the Ending**

Look at your last 2-3 sentences. Ask:

- Am I wrapping up too neatly?
- Is there a generic CTA I should cut?
- Can I end one sentence earlier?

**Step 7: Final Cleanup**

- Remove all em dashes (replace with commas, periods, colons, or parentheses)
- Check for bullet points (convert to prose)
- Remove any bold text
- Verify contractions feel natural
- Confirm geographic references are appropriately vague
- Check that product references use actual module names
- Ensure no institution or individual is named without approval

### 5.2 Common Edits You'll Make

Based on months of revision patterns, here are the most frequent edits:

**Opening Refinements:**

| Original Pattern | Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Quote + "That's what X told me" | Overused, generic | Lead with stat or situation instead |
| "Let me tell you about..." | Artificial setup | Just start the story |
| Generic observation as hook | Doesn't stop scroll | Find the surprising detail |
| "I recently had a conversation..." | Self-focused | Start with what they said or did |

**Middle Section Edits:**

| Original Pattern | Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "The result:" | Overly structured | Remove, let narrative flow |
| Explaining what a quote means | Redundant | Let quote speak for itself |
| Generic feature descriptions | Vague | Add specific module/feature names |
| Missing geographic context | Too abstract | Add casual location reference |
| No personal reaction | Feels robotic | Add brief authentic observation |

**Ending Edits:**

| Original Pattern | Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "What do you think?" | Engagement bait | Cut entirely |
| "If your team is struggling, you're not alone" | Generic comfort | Replace with specific insight |
| "That's the power of X" | Artificial wrap-up | End one sentence earlier |
| Generic partnership language | Corporate speak | Replace with specific forward look |
| Multiple closing thoughts | Dilutes impact | Pick one, cut the rest |

### 5.3 The "AI Slop" Filter

This is the final quality check. Read through and flag anything that feels:

**Overly Polished:**

- Perfect parallel structures
- Three-part lists that feel manufactured
- Transitions that announce themselves
- Sentences that sound like they came from a writing template

**Generic:**

- Could apply to any company in any industry
- Uses buzzwords instead of specific descriptions
- Makes claims without evidence
- Sounds like a press release

**Artificially Dramatic:**

- "And that's when everything changed"
- "But here's the thing"
- "The truth is"
- "It's not just about X, it's about Y"
- Two-sentence dramatic structures ("That's not a strategy. That's survival mode.")

**Examples of AI Slop Removal:**

❌ "That's not a strategy. That's survival mode."
✅ "Everyone goes into survival mode."

❌ "If your team is stuck in reactive mode, you're not alone. Most are. But it doesn't have to stay that way."
✅ "That level of intelligence is worth millions of dollars to your institution. And it's right there sitting in Atom."

❌ "It's become part of how they demonstrate value."
✅ Cut entirely, or replace with specific description

❌ "That came from their leadership during our check-in."
✅ Let the quote stand alone, or use parenthetical "(from their leadership)"

❌ "This is the kind of partnership we want with every customer. Real feedback. Honest conversations. And data that actually means something."
✅ "Helping institutions understand what's working in their own pipelines has been super rewarding for me."

- Personal voice present
- No generic CTA
- Product references use actual names
- No institutions/individuals named without approval
- Would say this out loud to a colleague

### 12.3 Emergency Fixes

If the hook is weak:
→ Find the most surprising number or quote and lead with it

If the middle drags:
→ Cut any sentence that explains what you're about to say

If the ending feels forced:
→ Delete the last sentence and see if it's better

## PART 14: VOICE CALIBRATION EXERCISES

### 14.1 The "Would I Say This?" Test

For every sentence, ask: Would I actually say this to a research administrator over
coffee?

Fails the test:

- "We're excited to announce our revolutionary new feature"
- "This game-changing capability will transform your workflow"
- "Leverage our cutting-edge AI to drive adoption"

Passes the test:

- "We just shipped something that came directly from customer feedback"
- "This should save about 30 hours per proposal"
- "Faculty actually use it, which is rare"

### 14.2 The "Press Release" Detector

If a sentence could appear in a press release, rewrite it.

Press release style:

"Atom Grants is proud to announce the launch of our Proposal Development module,
designed to streamline the grant writing process for research institutions."

Human style:

"We just shipped the Proposal Development module. It came from a year of
conversations with research teams who were stitching together five different tools
to manage one proposal."

### 14.3 The Specificity Upgrade

Take any vague statement and make it concrete.

| Vague | Specific |
|---|---|
| "Many institutions struggle with this" | "One team I spoke with has 2,400 faculty and 3 people managing outreach" |
| "The tool is easy to use" | "It took 10 minutes for faculty to get it. No training sessions needed." |
| "We help with compliance" | "Automated flags for CUI, foreign components, export control" |
| "Customers love it" | "66% activation rate. 1,000+ active users per week." |
| "It saves time" | "30 hours per planning document, now handled in the platform" |

### 14.4 The Ending Rewrite

Take any generic ending and make it specific.

| Generic | Specific |
|---|---|
| "What do you think?" | [Delete entirely] |
| "The future of research admin is here" | "And that's what we're building into the Proposal Development module." |
| "We're excited about what's next" | "Helping institutions understand what's working has been super rewarding for me." |
| "This is just the beginning" | "This will either be the downfall or the biggest breakthrough for RD teams within the next two years." |
| "Reach out if you want to learn more" | [Delete entirely, or end one sentence earlier] |

## PART 15: TROUBLESHOOTING GUIDE

### 15.1 Common Problems and Solutions

**Problem: Post feels too long**

Solutions:

- Cut any sentence that sets up what you're about to say
- Remove redundant context
- Delete the weakest paragraph
- See if the post works without the last 2-3 sentences

**Problem: Post feels too short**

Solutions:

- Add specific details (numbers, tools, processes)
- Include a quote from the conversation
- Add your personal reaction or observation
- Expand on the "why it matters" section

**Problem: Hook isn't working**

Solutions:

- Find the most surprising number and lead with it
- Start with the most unexpected quote
- Drop the reader directly into a situation
- Try a contrarian statement

**Problem: Middle section drags**

Solutions:

- Cut transitions ("But here's the thing...")
- Remove any sentence that explains what you're about to say
- Tighten the workaround description
- Make sure each paragraph adds new information

**Problem: Ending feels forced**

Solutions:

- Delete the last sentence and see if it's better
- Replace generic CTA with specific product reference
- Add personal reflection instead of summary
- Try an industry warning or prediction

**Problem: Sounds like marketing**

Solutions:

- Read it out loud
- Cut any word you wouldn't say to a colleague
- Replace buzzwords with plain language
- Remove superlatives (best, revolutionary, cutting-edge)

**Problem: Too many quotes**

Solutions:

- Pick the strongest one, cut the rest
- Integrate quotes into narrative flow
- Use parenthetical attribution instead of "she said"
- Let one powerful quote stand alone

**Problem: No personal voice**

Solutions:

- Add a brief reaction ("I was also expecting a more sophisticated answer")
- Include your perspective on what it means
- End with personal reflection
- Add "Sounds familiar right?" or similar conversational element

### 15.2 When to Break the Rules

These guidelines are defaults, not laws. Break them when:

**Using em dashes:**

- Never. This one is absolute.

**Using bullets:**

- Almost never. Only in comments, not posts.

**Exceeding 300 words:**

- When the story genuinely requires it and every word earns its place

**Naming institutions:**

- Only with explicit approval for case studies

**Using a quote as the hook:**

- When the quote is truly exceptional and unexpected

**Including a CTA:**

- When there's a specific, time-bound reason (event registration, etc.)

## PART 16: FINAL PRINCIPLES

### 16.1 The Hierarchy of Priorities

When in doubt, prioritize in this order:

1. **Authenticity** - Does it sound like a real person?
2. **Specificity** - Is it grounded in concrete details?
3. **Value** - Does the reader learn something?
4. **Voice** - Does it sound like you/Atom?
5. **Product** - Does it connect to what Atom does?

Never sacrifice #1-3 for #4-5.

### 16.2 The Core Beliefs Behind the Content

Every post should implicitly communicate:

- We understand research administration because we listen
- We build with institutions, not just for them
- AI should augment human work, not replace it
- Tools should fit academic workflows, not the other way around
- The people doing this work deserve better than what they have
- Honesty builds more trust than hype

### 16.3 The Ultimate Test

Before posting, ask:

"If a research administrator I've never met reads this, will they think: 'Finally,
someone who gets it'?"

- If yes, post it.
- If no, revise until the answer is yes.
